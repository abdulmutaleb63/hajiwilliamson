About the Journey

William Richard Williamson — later known as Abdullah Fadhil Al-Maslamani — was among the earliest British converts to Islam and lived most of his life across the Arabian Peninsula. His story intertwines seafaring adventure, faith, and the early oil history of the Gulf.

1872 — Birth in Bristol, England

Born in Bristol, he left school at thirteen to work at sea, driven by curiosity for the world beyond England.

1894 — Conversion to Islam

After arriving in Aden, he immersed himself in Islamic studies and embraced Islam, taking the name Abdullah Fadhil. He later performed Hajj and became known as “Haji.”

1900s–1920s — Life in Arabia

Settled between Kuwait, Basra, and Bahrain. Fluent in Arabic, he traded camels and horses, earning the respect of local tribes.

1920s–1930s — Work with Anglo-Persian Oil Company

Leveraging his deep knowledge of the Arab world, he served as a liaison between the British oil industry and Gulf sheikhs, helping shape early petroleum negotiations.

Later Years — Legacy

Known as “The English Arab”, he remained in Arabia until his final years, remembered by family and historians as a bridge between two worlds.

“Between the sea and the sands, a life is written in journeys.”
