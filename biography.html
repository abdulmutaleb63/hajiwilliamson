Early Life

Few areas in Britain have produced as many notable families in world history. The family name Williamson is one of those, believed to descend from the Strathclyde Britons — an ancient Gaelic/Celtic race. Among its many figures, one of the most notable was William Richard Williamson, later known as the Arabian adventurer.

Williamson was born in Bristol in 1872. Even as a boy, he showed a rebellious spirit and a hatred of injustice. While a pupil at Clifton School he repeatedly courted danger and punishment by climbing the famous Clifton suspension bridge.

To the Sea

At thirteen, Williamson left school and joined a tea-clipper bound for Australia. Life at sea was brutal: flogging, climbing the mast in gales, and the harshness of shipboard discipline. But instead of breaking him, it fueled his love of adventure.

He deserted in California, working on a ranch south of Los Angeles. There he mastered cowboy skills — bronco-breaking, gun-slinging — yet refused to waste his earnings on vice. A strong belief in God began to grow within him.

Gold Trails & Hardships

Fired by tales of gold, he and a companion set off to Nevada, only to be robbed of mule, money, and even their shoes. Undeterred, Williamson pressed on, taking odd jobs before returning to San Francisco. He became a sailor once more, voyaging around Cape Horn and later attempting to work on the Panama Canal.

Suffering malaria, bankruptcy, and harsh winters, Williamson survived by forming a traveling vaudeville troupe and even tried his hand at boxing, winning his first three bouts.

Adventures in the Pacific

Williamson sailed on the Sitka Brave through the Bering Straits and later traded sea-cucumbers in the Caroline Islands. His fortunes turned when Spanish authorities arrested him, accusing him of gun-running, and he was imprisoned in Manila under appalling conditions.

He endured torture, forced labor, and near-death experiences before escaping to the US consulate. Disguised, he fled to Hong Kong and continued his life at sea.

Conversion to Islam

In Bombay, Williamson’s spiritual yearning grew. Aboard the SS Siam, he discovered a book by Imam Abdullah Quilliam, Sheikh al-Islam of the British Isles. The clarity of Islam resonated deeply with him.

In Aden he formally embraced Islam, took the name Abdullah Fadhil, and later performed Hajj in 1894. His life’s direction was forever changed.

Life in Arabia

Settling in Kuwait, Zubair, and Basra, he learned Arabic and traded camels and horses. He became respected among Gulf tribes and merchants, even amusing locals with European inventions like a phonograph and penny-farthing bicycle.

He lived as a Bedouin trader, balancing the life of an Englishman with that of an Arab Muslim.

Pearl Diving & Oil

Haji Abdullah joined great pearling expeditions in the Gulf, commanding his dhow Fath al-Khayr. Though he never struck riches, his adventures brought him to Damascus for further Islamic study.

Later he worked with the Anglo-Persian Oil Company, guiding negotiations in Abu Dhabi and helping Imperial Airways chart routes across Arabia — shaping the future of the region.

Final Years

Retiring to Basra, Haji raised children and grandchildren, sharing tales of his extraordinary life. He frequented the Ashar Mosque, studied religion, and indulged in penny Western novels.

He passed away in 1958, remembered as “The English Arab,” a man whose life bridged East and West, faith and trade, tradition and change.
